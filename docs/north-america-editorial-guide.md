# North America Geopolitics Editorial Guide

## Purpose

Y-SQRE North America coverage should explain how power, policy, trade, security
and technology decisions in the United States, Canada and Mexico shape economic
opportunity. The writing should help a serious reader understand what changed,
why it matters and what to watch next.

## House Style

This guide adapts practical principles from Ann Handley's *Everybody Writes*
and William Zinsser's *On Writing Well*. It is an original editorial brief, not
a reproduction of either book.

- Lead with the news or insight. Put the main actor and action near the start.
- Respect the reader's time. Remove background that does not help the reader
  interpret the issue.
- Prefer clear, short words to institutional jargon.
- Use active voice when the actor is known.
- Explain technical terms on first use. Never assume specialist knowledge.
- Keep the tone calm, precise and independent. Avoid hype and moralizing.
- Use numbers when they clarify scale, but explain what the numbers mean.
- Separate verified fact, informed analysis and open questions.
- Give the reader a reason to continue in the first paragraph.
- End with a forward-looking signal: the decision, event or data point to watch.

## Article Structure

### Headline

Make one useful promise. Name the change and its consequence where possible.

Example:

`Mexico's manufacturing push is changing the map of North American trade`

### Standfirst

In one or two sentences, explain the core shift and why it matters.

### Opening

Answer three questions within the first three paragraphs:

1. What changed?
2. Why does it matter now?
3. Who is affected?

### Body

Use short sections with descriptive subheadings:

1. The development
2. The forces behind it
3. The economic or strategic consequence
4. The counterargument or constraint
5. What to watch next

### Ending

Finish with a concrete signal rather than a summary. Examples include a vote,
an election, a tariff decision, an earnings call, a treaty review or a data
release.

## Coverage Pillars

### Trade and Industry

Cover supply chains, nearshoring, tariffs, industrial policy, critical
minerals, energy links and the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement.

### Security and Alliances

Cover defence priorities, NATO commitments, Arctic strategy, border security
and the region's role in wider geopolitical competition.

### Technology and Capital

Cover semiconductors, artificial intelligence infrastructure, energy demand,
investment controls and the flow of capital into strategic industries.

### Domestic Policy With Global Consequences

Cover elections, migration, regulation and fiscal decisions when they alter
trade, investment or international relationships.

## Editorial Checklist

Before publishing, ask:

1. Is the central point clear in the opening paragraph?
2. Does every paragraph add new information or interpretation?
3. Can any long phrase become a short one?
4. Have we identified the actor behind each important action?
5. Have we removed jargon, filler and unsupported certainty?
6. Have we represented a credible opposing view or constraint?
7. Are facts sourced and current as of the publication date?
8. Does the ending tell the reader what to watch next?

## Starter Topics

- How the next USMCA review could shape North American supply chains
- Why the Arctic is becoming a larger strategic question for Canada and the US
- The investment consequences of tighter technology controls
- Mexico's role in the next phase of manufacturing nearshoring
- How energy infrastructure connects North American industrial policy
